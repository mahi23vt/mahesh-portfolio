Defense · Process Analysis
A system built to do more, doing far less
35 → 250+/day
+

Problem

A defense establishment was generating only 35 visitor passes per day through a system designed to handle far higher volume. No single team could explain why — the software worked, the hardware worked, the integration worked. Individually.

Approach

Mapped the entire flow end to end — across the internal platform, a third-party system, and the hardware layer — rather than auditing each piece in isolation. The bottleneck wasn't a failure. It was a handoff nobody owned.

Result

Proposed and validated targeted process and integration changes. Throughput increased to 250+ passes per day — a 7× improvement — and the redesigned architecture opened the door to further integrations that weren't previously possible.

What it required

Cross-system diagnosis, stakeholder trust in a high-security environment, and the discipline to fix the process before touching the technology.

Critical Infrastructure · Multi-vendor Integration
Three vendors, zero shared history, one platform
3 → 1 platform
+

Problem

A national critical infrastructure site needed frictionless, camera-based access control — zero physical biometric contact. Three independent technology vendors held the pieces: a video management system, a secondary surveillance platform, and an access control system. None had ever integrated with each other before.

Approach

Acted as the sole coordination point across all three vendor teams. Designed the integration architecture — an event-driven model where facial recognition triggers were subscribed to in real time, mapped to physical doors, and cross-referenced against an active blacklist.

Result

Delivered a unified, real-time security platform — three previously unconnected systems operating as one. A face on camera could open the correct door in real time; a blacklisted individual triggered an immediate alert across the facility.

What it required

Reading three different API documentation sets, resolving assumptions that didn't align, and keeping three external teams moving toward a single outcome with no shared management chain.

Education · Requirements & Product Design
Designing trust into a child safety platform
5,000+ users
+

Problem

A new school wanted to differentiate itself on student safety — every movement of every child tracked and communicated to parents in real time, with handover permitted only to pre-approved guardians, whether at the school gate or on a moving bus.

Approach

Elicited requirements through structured discovery and Figma wireframes, translating an ambitious vision into a concrete specification: dual biometric authentication at school gates, fingerprint verification on bus tablets, guardian whitelisting, real-time GPS tracking, and SMS alerts at every movement event.

Result

Delivered a complete platform across mobile and web — a live principal dashboard, automated parent notifications, and a verified handover chain. The system became the school's primary safety differentiator in the market.

What it required

Designing for an emotional outcome — a parent's peace of mind — not just a technical one. Every edge case (an unrecognised guardian, a missed scan) had to fail safely.

Defense Research · Scope Recovery
A project with no end date, given one
Signed off & closed
+

Problem

A new platform rollout for a defense research client had spiralled. An on-site developer was receiving incremental change requests daily, with no defined scope, no documented end state, and no closure in sight.

Approach

Took direct ownership of client communication, removing the developer from that role entirely. Forced a single structured session where the client had to articulate every remaining requirement at once — rather than one new request per day.

Result

Baselined the full scope, delivered against it, and secured formal client sign-off — closing a project that had no defined end date. The relationship was strong enough afterward that it led directly to a second, separate engagement.

What it required

Giving structure to a client who didn't realise they lacked it — without making them feel mismanaged. Firm, not adversarial.
